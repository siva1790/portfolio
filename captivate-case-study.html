Enterprise service design case study

Captivate: turning a static visit request form into a shared coordination workspace.

Captivate is an internal enterprise platform used to plan client and leadership visits across office locations. I combined stakeholder walkthroughs, heuristic evaluation and workflow research to identify why users relied on calls, email and chat—and translated those findings into a progressive, multi-location planning and service-tracking experience.

RoleUX Researcher / Interaction Designer
MethodsStakeholder interviews, walkthrough, heuristic evaluation
ScopeRequester journey and shared visit coordination
DeliverableInformation architecture, user flows and prototype
01 · Challenge

The context

A visit looked like one request, but operationally behaved like a network of changing dependencies.

Client and leadership visits require several independent teams to coordinate spaces, technology, badging, food and refreshments, stationery and experience support. A visit may involve one office, multiple offices in the same city, or several cities. Each meeting can have different attendees, timings, rooms and service requirements.

The existing experience treated this complexity as a single, largely tabular form. Users were expected to provide information before it was available, while service providers continued coordinating through email, official chat channels and scoping calls.

The design challenge was to make an evolving visit understandable and actionable for everyone involved, while keeping the request light enough to start before every detail was known.

Business and user tension

  • Requesters wanted to reserve support early, even when guest lists and agendas were incomplete.
  • Service teams needed reliable details before confirming availability.
  • Coordinators needed a complete view across locations and service owners.
  • The organization wanted to reduce manual follow-up and avoid unnecessary scoping calls.
02 · Research process

Triangulating the problem

I studied the product from three complementary angles.

No single method could explain both the interface friction and the operational breakdown behind it. I combined behavioural evidence from stakeholders with an interface-level evaluation and an end-to-end walkthrough of the existing process.

1

Stakeholder interviews

Understood how requesters, coordinators and service teams exchange information, make decisions and recover when information changes.

2

Existing product walkthrough

Mapped the request lifecycle from initial entry to assignment, service monitoring, notifications and visit completion.

3

Heuristic evaluation

Assessed navigation, visibility of status, error prevention, content hierarchy, accessibility and consistency with brand standards.

Stakeholder walkthrough: how the work actually happened

The walkthrough exposed a gap between the system’s apparent linearity and the real operational lifecycle. The request was only the beginning; coordination continued across several people and channels.

Existing workflow

Coordinator schedules a scoping call
Request is assigned to service owners
Teams update Captivate, email and chat
Requester manually tracks confirmation
Timing

Requests arrived anywhere from two months to two days before a visit.

The workflow needed to support both early planning and time-sensitive requests without pretending all information would be known at the start.

Operations

Service teams worked to a 48-hour review expectation.

The product did not clearly distinguish response time from the minimum lead time required to deliver an individual service.

Behaviour

Users entered dummy data to finish the form.

This pointed to premature data requirements rather than carelessness or lack of compliance.

Coordination

Status updates lived across chat, email and the admin tool.

Captivate lacked action-driven status updates and a reliable shared summary.

Heuristic evaluation

The interface review showed that visual and interaction issues reinforced the workflow problems. Users could not clearly understand where they were, what was required now, what could wait, or what would happen after submission.

Match with the real world

A multi-location, multi-meeting visit was represented as one flat form.

Services became difficult to associate with the correct office, date or audience.

User control and freedom

Editing time, venue, catering or attendee details was not sufficiently supported.

Error prevention

Content hierarchy, contrast, imagery, icon use and brand patterns needed improvement.

Synthesis: the recurring themes

I grouped the findings by underlying cause rather than by screen. This prevented the redesign from becoming a collection of isolated interface fixes.

A visit contains locations and sessions
Services have independent owners and statuses
Changes are normal, not exceptional
03 · Problems identified

From observations to design challenges

The findings resolved into six connected product problems.

Each problem was written in operational terms so that the design response could improve both requester usability and service-team coordination.

1. Premature completeness

Users had to provide detailed guest and service information before it was known.

→

Progressive commitment

Separate a preliminary support request from later attendee and service details.

2. Flat information model

One form could not elegantly represent several cities, offices and meetings.

→

Visit hierarchy

Structure information as Visit → Location → Session → Service.

3. Generic service capture

All services were requested in one place despite requiring different details.

→

Conditional configuration

Show questions appropriate to rooms, technology, catering and access.

4. One headcount for everything

Meeting room, lunch and refreshments could involve different numbers of people.

→

Session-level attendance

Assign attendees and quantities to specific sessions and services.

5. Fragmented status

Overall progress depended on manual email and chat updates.

→

Service-level tracking

Expose owner, status, questions and confirmation for every service request.

6. Changes broke the process

Updates after submission were difficult to communicate and revalidate.

→

Targeted revalidation

Reopen only the services affected by a material change.

04 · Design strategy

Reframing the product

The redesign positioned Captivate as a shared visit workspace.

The redesign needed to support the lifecycle before, during and after submission. I used three principles to maintain this direction across screens.

PRINCIPLE 01

Ask for the right detail at the right time.

Make early submission possible, then use clear deadlines and readiness indicators for information that can follow.

PRINCIPLE 02

Organize the interface like the visit itself.

Keep locations, sessions, attendees and services connected so users can understand dependencies without a scoping call.

PRINCIPLE 03

Make ownership and consequence visible.

Every action should explain what was sent, who is reviewing it and whether a change affects confirmation.

The new model separates information readiness, service confirmation and actual visit status, so progress is tied to the work happening behind the scenes.

05 · Design solution

Research translated into interaction

Each major interface decision directly addressed a research finding.

1

A task-oriented “My visits” dashboard

The old experience did not give requesters a useful summary after submission. The new dashboard prioritizes visits requiring attention, services awaiting teams and confirmed visits.

Evidence

Status updates were fragmented across email and chat.

Design decision

Show attention required, confirmation progress and the next primary action.

Expected effect

Reduce “What is happening with my request?” follow-ups.

2

Preliminary request before detailed planning

Users can create a workspace with basic client, purpose, dates, locations, approximate attendance and broad support categories. The interface explicitly states that services are not yet confirmed.

Evidence

Users entered dummy data when details were unavailable.

Design decision

Distinguish “required now” from “can be completed later.”

Expected effect

Improve data quality without delaying early coordination.

3

A persistent visit workspace with clear navigation

A compact visit context remains visible above tabs for Overview, Attendees, Locations & sessions, Services, and Review & send. Each tab then presents its own header and action.

Attendees

Manage visitor, host and local attendee details in one place.

4

Locations and sessions as the planning backbone

The itinerary introduces the missing session layer beneath each office. Rooms, catering, attendees and technology can now belong to the meeting where they are actually needed.

Evidence

“Multiple touchpoints” meant leadership meetings across offices and cities.

Design decision

Represent every session with date, time, attendance, format and services.

Expected effect

Remove ambiguity that previously required a scoping call.

5

Conditional service configuration

Selected services reveal only their relevant details. Technology asks for conferencing platform and technician support. Catering asks for quantity and dietary requirements. Visitor access is generated from the guest list.

Evidence

Service teams needed different operational information.

Design decision

Explain why a question is required and recommend services from visit context.

Expected effect

Capture micro-details accurately while keeping the interface manageable.

6

Independent service statuses and focused questions

Service confirmation is separated from information completeness. Teams can confirm, review, request information or decline support at the service level.

Evidence

Different teams approved and updated their own services.

Design decision

Calculate overall progress from the status of individual services.

Expected effect

Create a reliable shared source of truth without hiding partial progress.

7

Service Library and contextual Help

The global Service Library explains availability, lead time and required information. Help is organized around user tasks and surfaces unresolved items from the active visit before offering escalation.

Evidence

Users needed inspiration and clarification without leaving the planning flow.

Design decision

Connect learning content directly to visit and session actions.

Expected effect

Reduce broad consultations while preserving support for genuinely complex visits.

07 · Outcome & next steps

What changed

The redesign converted operational ambiguity into visible structure, ownership and next actions.

The current outcome is a detailed interaction model and clickable prototype. Because the product has not yet been measured in production, I frame impact as design improvements and testable hypotheses rather than claiming unverified business results.

Progressive planning

Requesters can begin with known details and complete the visit plan as information becomes available.

Shared status and ownership

The visit workspace brings service progress, responsibilities and next actions into one place.

More focused follow-up

Contextual questions and structured service details reduce the need for broad scoping conversations.

Next steps

  • Run task-based usability testing with requesters planning both single-location and multi-city visits.
  • Test whether service teams receive enough information to avoid a scoping call.
  • Validate terminology such as preliminary support, service confirmation and visit status.
  • Measure completion quality, clarification volume, time to first service response and status-related follow-up.
  • Define the integration boundary between Captivate and the backend admin tool before engineering handoff.
