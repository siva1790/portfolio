UX leadership case study

Deloitte.com modernization: building a UX-led delivery model for DMOD adoption.

Deloitte's Marketing Excellence organization needed a more consistent way to create public-facing Deloitte.com pages, with clearer brand application, content strategy, analytics planning and a smoother delivery process. As UX Lead and Specialist Assistant Manager, my focus was to move a 40+ member team from fragmented, stakeholder-led page creation to a sprint-based, design-system-driven workflow over 1.5 years.

01 · Challenge

The context

The team was creating pages, but the process was hard to repeat at scale.

Deloitte.com page creation had grown into a high-stakes collaboration between strategic marketers, content specialists, designers, developers and stakeholders. The legacy design system gave teams significant freedom for custom coding, and templates often acted as loose guidance rather than reliable standards.

As a result, page quality depended heavily on individual interpretation. Some pages did not consistently follow brand guidelines for color, typography or layout. Developers and stakeholders sometimes shaped page design directly, while final decisions were often made without enough brand, UX or content-strategy context.

01

Inconsistent brand execution

Legacy templates allowed too much interpretation, which created variation in layout, type, color and experience quality.

02

Stakeholder-led decisions

Final page choices were often made by people close to the business request, but not always close to brand, UX or content strategy.

03

Limited measurement foundation

Metrics and interaction tracking were not consistently planned at component level, making performance learning harder.

04

Long production timelines

Unclear intake, handoffs and approvals created rework between marketers, creatives, developers and stakeholders.

05

DMOD was still evolving

DMOD was the direction, but the team needed a transition plan before every official component and template was ready.

06

UX maturity gap

The team needed a shared understanding of why story, hierarchy, layout and interaction choices affected page reach.

The bigger challenge was not individual page design. It was giving teams a consistent way to make page decisions across many different requests.

02 · Role

Leadership scope

The UX role sat across process, people, design-system adoption and measurement.

The role required a more structured UX way of working while still supporting the monthly flow of Deloitte.com page requests. That meant hands-on design leadership, stakeholder education, process design, team enablement and a practical transition plan for DMOD.

Educate

Raised UX understanding

Helped marketers and creatives understand page storytelling, hierarchy, user intent, content sequencing and why standardized design improves trust and reach.

Operationalize

Created the delivery model

Introduced a sprint-based intake and delivery process with clearer kickoff, readiness, handoff and approval checkpoints.

Enable

Built team capability

Hired and onboarded two UX designers, giving the team more capacity for content strategy, component mapping and page design.

Systemize

Prepared DMOD adoption

Created pseudo-DMOD components and templates in Figma so the team could start practicing the new model before the official system was fully released.

Measure

Connected design to analytics

Planned component-level tracking across Adobe Analytics and the move to CJA, supporting better performance review and A/B testing.

Automate

Reduced repetitive effort

Created AI-powered internal tools and a brand-guidelines Figma plugin to reduce production load and improve designer efficiency.

03 · Transformation roadmap

1.5 years · 3 phases

The work unfolded in three phases: create the process, support adoption and refine the workflow.

The rollout was phased so the team could keep delivering active page requests while gradually shifting to a more disciplined, DMOD-aligned way of working.

Phase 1Process creation and adoption

Established UX education, page intake, template selection, content mapping and a pseudo-DMOD Figma system so the team could practice the new workflow before full DMOD release.

Phase 2Workflow refinement

Adjusted the process based on team feedback, clarified definitions of ready and done, improved approvals and strengthened collaboration between marketers, UX, copy, visual design and development.

Phase 3Measurement and automation

Embedded analytics thinking into component planning, supported A/B testing, and introduced internal tools that reduced repetitive production effort for creatives and designers.

04 · UX delivery process

From request to published page

Page creation became a clearer UX-led operating workflow.

The process defined when UX should enter, what information was required before each stage, who owned each activity, and what counted as done before the work could move forward. This reduced ambiguity and gave the 40+ member organization a shared way to collaborate.

05 · DMOD adoption

Design-system transition

A pseudo-DMOD Figma system made the new way of working easier to practice before the official system was fully ready.

The official DMOD design system was still in progress. Waiting for every final artifact would have slowed the transition, so a practical interim system was created in Figma to mirror the expected DMOD templates, components, selection rules and content constraints.

Pseudo-DMOD component library

Reusable Figma components gave designers and marketers a faster way to evaluate page structure without custom-building every request.

HeroCampaign message, audience context, CTA priority
Story blockLong-form explanation with content hierarchy
CardsScannable offerings, themes or related content
CTA moduleConversion path and analytics event planning

Template and content mapping rules

The team learned how to choose a template based on content purpose instead of stakeholder preference alone.

GoalAwareness, engagement, lead path or event support
StoryWhat the user needs to understand first, next and last
EvidenceProof points, imagery, video and supporting data
MeasureInteractions that should be tracked after launch

Why this mattered: the interim system reduced transition risk. By the time the official DMOD system was released, the team had already practiced the mindset, handoffs and component-selection behavior needed for adoption.

06 · Measurement

Analytics-aware experience design

Analytics planning moved earlier in the page process.

The team used Adobe Analytics and later moved toward CJA. Tracking needs were discussed during component planning instead of being treated as a launch task, which made it easier to measure important interactions after launch. It also gave the team a better starting point for A/B testing and future component choices.

Plan

Define page intent

Clarify the marketing goal, user need and desired behavior.

Map

Select components

Choose templates and components based on content type and interaction purpose.

Track

Instrument interactions

Plan tracking for CTAs, navigation paths and key engagement points.

Learn

Review performance

Use Adobe Analytics / CJA data to understand how components perform.

Optimize

Test and improve

Run A/B tests and refine future component choices based on evidence.

07 · Automation

Operational efficiency

Automation took repetitive production work out of the process.

As the UX process became more established, internal tooling became a practical way to reduce manual effort and keep designers focused on decisions that needed their judgment.

~60% less production time

Programmatic ad generator

Built an AI-powered internal tool that turns a message and image into multi-resolution banners, reducing repetitive resizing and production work.

~10% designer efficiency gain

Brand-guidelines Figma plugin

Built a Figma plugin with Claude that gives designers quick access to brand colors, contrast-ratio checks, hex values and icons, reducing tab-switching during design work.
